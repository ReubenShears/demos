The screening problem

Questions, answered

Everything you're wondering before you switch

Isn't letting candidates use AI just making it too easy? +

The opposite. When everyone has AI, the easy answers stop being a differentiator — and judgment, speed, and communication rise to the surface. That's exactly the signal you can't fake and can't get from a banned-tools test.

How is this different from our existing coding test? +

Legacy tests measure whether someone can survive without their tools. JAI measures how well they perform with them — on realistic, job-shaped problems — so your score actually predicts on-the-job performance.

Will this work for my specific stack and roles? +

JAI assessments focus on the durable skills every engineering role needs — judgment, build speed, communication — so they translate across languages, frameworks, and seniority levels rather than testing one narrow syntax.

What if the results don't match how people actually perform? +

That's the whole design goal. By scoring the behaviors that carry into the job — not memorized trivia — JAI's signal is built to line up with real output. If a screen isn't surfacing genuine judgment, it isn't doing its job.

Is it fair to every candidate? +

Everyone gets the same tools and the same realistic problems — the way they'd work on day one. That's fairer than a format that rewards whoever grinded the most puzzle prep, and it gives strong builders a place to shine.

How long does it take to get started? +

Sign in with LinkedIn or GitHub and you're an early adopter in minutes — no lengthy procurement or engineering lift to run your first AI-native screen.

How soon will we see better hiring decisions? +

From your very first screen. Because JAI shows judgment, speed, and communication side by side, hiring teams get a clearer, more defensible read on candidates immediately — not after months of calibration.
